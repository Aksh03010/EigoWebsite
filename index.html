Positionality Statement & Identity

An exploration of cultural heritage, personal context, and scholarly interests.

Welcome! This page outlines my academic positionality—the unique intersection of culture, experiences, and background that shapes how I read, write, and engage with the world.

Who I Am

As a writer and student, my work is directly influenced by my cultural heritage and the community in which I was raised. I grew up navigating multiple cultural boundaries, which fostered a deep fascination with how language serves as both a barrier and a bridge.

My academic interests lie at the intersection of media study, rhetoric, and creative writing. I believe that understanding our positionality is crucial to academic inquiry, as it allows us to identify our assumptions and open ourselves up to diverse ways of knowing.

My Hobbies & Interests

Outside of writing and research, I spend my time exploring photography, which allows me to tell stories visually. I also enjoy hiking and immersing myself in nature, finding that the quiet of the outdoors provides space to process complex concepts we discuss in the classroom.

Section II

Conditioned for Chaos

Human beings possess a terrifying ability to adapt to cruelty, but I think that the true tragedy of survival is the process of unlearning the defenses that keep us alive at some point. In Michelle Good’s Five Little Indians, this psychological trap is clear in Kenny, whose trauma responses actively destroy his ability to experience safety. Kenny’s inability to accept stillness stems from his brutal childhood conditioning at the Mission, a psychological phenomenon that perfectly mirrors the paranoia viewers experience when an aggressively violent narrative like Vinland Saga suddenly turns peaceful, ultimately explaining why he repeatedly abandons his own moments of stability.

______

To understand what Kenny's life is like as an adult, you have to understand the baseline of survival he is forced to establish as a child. As a student at the mission school, Kenny lives with a constant threat of physical harm, a perpetual atmosphere of fear, and a necessity to be constantly vigilant. If someone as young as Kenny lets their guard down or believes that there will ever be peace, it can prove fatal. The survivors of this school system have to live with the reality of having “not a day without fear” (Good 106). Because of how his brain is wired due to his anticipation of cruelty from humans and the environment around them, Kenny experiences chaos as his norm. When he is provided a safe, peaceful existence as an adult, this stillness doesn't feel like a sanctuary, but a terrifying void of unknowing.

My personal experience with media pacing shapes my understanding of this terrifying relationship with stillness. While watching the anime Vinland Saga, a story heavily defined by the constant warfare, brutality, and combat of Vikings, I experience a sudden and jarring psychological shift during its much slower and peaceful second season. Instead of feeling relaxed by the calm, I find myself feeling incredibly uncomfortable and nervous. Because I am so conditioned as a viewer to expect violence, when it suddenly disappears into silence, it feels suspicious. I become completely paranoid, convinced that something terrible is about to happen at any second. This anxiety perfectly shows what Kenny experiences in the real world; he cannot enjoy the quiet because his mind is permanently bracing for an ambush.

Kenny's deep-rooted paranoia determines his avoidance of his feelings throughout the entire book. His paranoia causes him to sabotage his own happiness by escaping. We can see this clearly when Lucy gives birth to Kendra and they get married. Instead of enjoying this stability, Kenny gets "the itch" and leaves his new wife and baby for a job at a remote logging camp. This self-destructive habit is demonstrated again later in the book when he comes back to help Lucy. After sharing a rare, perfect day of peace, Kenny wakes up the next morning and silently runs away once more. Just like a viewer nervously waiting for a sudden attack during a quiet television scene, Kenny abandons his family because he believes that a calm environment makes it inevitable that the next traumatic event is close behind.

Ultimately, escaping the physical walls of the Mission does not save Kenny, because his mind remains entirely conditioned for chaos. The institutional abuse he suffers as a child permanently alters his psychological baseline, turning the beautiful, quiet moments of life into sources of unbearable anxiety. By looking through the lens of media like Vinland Saga, it becomes clear that for a severely traumatized person, peace is not comforting; it is a warning sign. Kenny stays constantly moving because he is too afraid of what might happen if he finally stands still.

Written Whispering Culture

Decolonizing the Page: Power Dynamics in Modern Rhetoric.

This written response explores power dynamics in academic writing, questioning who decides what constitutes "proper" English and how language standards can marginalize diverse voices.

Language as a Mechanism of Exclusion

Academic settings have long maintained rigid guidelines for composition, prioritizing Standard Written English (SWE) as the default voice of intellectual credibility. However, this standard is not politically neutral. It is deeply entwined with historical systems of class and cultural dominance. By enforcing a singular standard, institutions often exclude or delegitimize the linguistic styles, dialects, and expressive patterns of historically marginalized groups.

Decolonizing the page requires us to expand our rhetorical definitions. It asks educators and students to value diverse dialects, multi-lingual code-switching, and alternative narrative structures as equally valid forms of analytical writing.

Classroom Dialogue & Reflective Poetry

During our in-class debates, we observed how our peers' vernacular styles rich in cultural context communicated deep sociological concepts with greater resonance than formal prose. Language is alive; trying to lock it in a singular template limits the expressive capacity of our scholarship.
