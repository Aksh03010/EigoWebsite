Positionality Statement & Identity

An exploration of cultural heritage, personal context, and scholarly interests.

Who I Am

Hi, I'm Akshit Mesa, a high school student. My life has been a blur of motion, shaped by deep frequencies and the vastness of the cosmos. I have a background of moving every three years, from India to New York City, and then to Canada. Living across three distinct countries has given me a fluid definition of identity. While I deeply respect the Hindu and Christian traditions of my parents and continue to participate in their cultural practices, I am an atheist. Growing up in NYC, a city I love, has had a lasting impact on my perspective. The city, where my closest connections remain, has been a defining force. For me, culture is a living, breathing remix.

Music, Creative Media & Ambitions

Music has been my constant companion since I was four. It started with singing and playing the recorder, then evolved to the flute, clarinet, and percussion. This passion led me to digital music production with LMMS and UTAU, influenced by J-Pop, J-Rock, and Vocaloid. The Japanese artist Ado has been a vital part of this journey. Her raw vocal power and distinct artistry helped me navigate stress and anxiety. Attending her concert was a milestone that reinforced my deep connection to her work.

My goal is to contribute to Astrophysics and Aerospace Engineering. A lifelong fascination with the universe drives this ambition. I want to combine my technical skills with my creative problem-solving abilities to fulfill my childhood dreams. By combining my knowledge of coding, robotics, and photography, I plan to analyze astronomical data or engineer new spacecrafts. I hope to help humans understand the frontier beyond our planet. Through my work, I aim to make meaningful contributions to the field and play a role in advancing our understanding of the universe.

Participating in this NBE course, I approach texts with an appreciation for how stories, much like soundwaves, evolve, collide, and resonate across generations.

"Culture isn't a monument set in stone; it’s a soundwave of speech moving across generations, colliding with other waves to amplify some parts and cancel out others."

Section II

Conditioned for Chaos

Human beings possess a terrifying ability to adapt to cruelty, but I think that the true tragedy of survival is the process of unlearning the defenses that keep us alive at some point.

In Michelle Good’s Five Little Indians, this psychological trap is clear in Kenny, whose trauma responses actively destroy his ability to experience safety. Kenny’s inability to accept stillness stems from his brutal childhood conditioning at the Mission, a psychological phenomenon that perfectly mirrors the paranoia viewers experience when an aggressively violent narrative like Vinland Saga suddenly turns peaceful, ultimately explaining why he repeatedly abandons his own moments of stability.

To understand what Kenny's life is like as an adult, you have to understand the baseline of survival he is forced to establish as a child. As a student at the mission school, Kenny lives with a constant threat of physical harm, a perpetual atmosphere of fear, and a necessity to be constantly vigilant. If someone as young as Kenny lets their guard down or believes that there will ever be peace, it can prove fatal. The survivors of this school system have to live with the reality of having “not a day without fear” (Good 106). Because of how his brain is wired due to his anticipation of cruelty from humans and the environment around them, Kenny experiences chaos as his norm. When he is provided a safe, peaceful existence as an adult, this stillness doesn't feel like a sanctuary, but a terrifying void of unknowing.

My personal experience with media pacing shapes my understanding of this terrifying relationship with stillness. While watching the anime Vinland Saga, a story heavily defined by the constant warfare, brutality, and combat of Vikings, I experience a sudden and jarring psychological shift during its much slower and peaceful second season. Instead of feeling relaxed by the calm, I find myself feeling incredibly uncomfortable and nervous. Because I am so conditioned as a viewer to expect violence, when it suddenly disappears into silence, it feels suspicious. I become completely paranoid, convinced that something terrible is about to happen at any second. This anxiety perfectly shows what Kenny experiences in the real world; he cannot enjoy the quiet because his mind is permanently bracing for an ambush.

Kenny's deep-rooted paranoia determines his avoidance of his feelings throughout the entire book. His paranoia causes him to sabotage his own happiness by escaping. We can see this clearly when Lucy gives birth to Kendra and they get married. Instead of enjoying this stability, Kenny gets "the itch" and leaves his new wife and baby for a job at a remote logging camp. This self-destructive habit is demonstrated again later in the book when he comes back to help Lucy. After sharing a rare, perfect day of peace, Kenny wakes up the next morning and silently runs away once more. Just like a viewer nervously waiting for a sudden attack during a quiet television scene, Kenny abandons his family because he believes that a calm environment makes it inevitable that the next traumatic event is close behind.

Ultimately, escaping the physical walls of the Mission does not save Kenny, because his mind remains entirely conditioned for chaos. The institutional abuse he suffers as a child permanently alters his psychological baseline, turning the beautiful, quiet moments of life into sources of unbearable anxiety. By looking through the lens of media like Vinland Saga, it becomes clear that for a severely traumatized person, peace is not comforting; it is a warning sign. Kenny stays constantly moving because he is too afraid of what might happen if he finally stands still.

Key Takeaway

The constant urge to move and avoid stillness stems from the deep-seated trauma of institutional abuse. In the absence of a safe environment, the mind learns that danger often follows peace, creating a perpetual state of anxiety.

Section III

Whispering Culture

In the cold and pure air of the early mornings at the residential school, Saul, in the barn picking up frozen horse turds for hockey, is resisting oppression. Saul Indian Horse in Indian Horse by Richard Wagamese is a character who keeps on defying norms and systems silently. He refuses to forget his Ojibway language, and he keeps his land and home close to him while breaking the rules to learn hockey.

Saul falls in love with the game of hockey at the age of eight in the school. He yearns to learn and play the game. However, he is forced by the rules of the school to stay back and watch others play, but Saul does not accept these rules. He convinces Father Leboutillier to let him clear the rink in the mornings and takes advantage of this to learn hockey, even if it means breaking the rules. He continues to practice regularly, watch hockey games, and learn from them. This act of quiet resistance helps Saul not only have fun, but it lets him live.

Language makes up a big portion of who you are. It is no different for Saul, but the residential schools have a goal of destroying the identity of Indigenous people and replacing it with their own. Saul, however, refuses to forget his language; he resists the bleaching of his culture and identity. Saul does not stop talking in his language and continues to resist the oppression of the schools. God’s Lake is a place that Saul never forgets and always keeps in his heart. Close to the end of the book, Saul realizes that “[he has] come to learn to carry this place with[him]" (Wagamese 205). This major part of Saul and his culture is also an example of Saul's quiet resistance.

Throughout the novel, we see Saul's acts of resistance that we sometimes might not even notice are displayed by him as the reasons and ways he survives "St Jerome's, hell on earth" (Wagamese 78). His sneaky tricks to have Father Leboutillier let him play hockey, his perseverance to preserve his language, and his act of carrying God's Lake with him are all quiet resistance against oppressive systems and norms.
